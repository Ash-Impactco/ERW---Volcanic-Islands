## **1. RESEARCH HYPOTHESIS**

### **1.1 Primary Hypothesis**

**H₁:** Application of finely ground basaltic rock (<100 µm median particle size) to acidic volcanic soils (pH 5.2-6.0, high organic matter 6-12%) on São Miguel Island at rates of 2.7-50 t/ha will:

1. **Enhance silicate mineral dissolution rates** by 5-15x relative to neutral pH agricultural soils (reference: pH 7.0, <2% OM), driven by:
   - Proton-promoted dissolution kinetics (Lasaga, 1981; White & Brantley, 2003)
   - Organic ligand-enhanced weathering (Drever & Stillings, 1997)
   - Elevated soil pCO₂ from microbial respiration (10-100x atmospheric)

2. **Increase soil alkalinity production** by 2-6 meq/kg/yr, measurable via:
   - Soil solution bicarbonate (HCO₃⁻) accumulation
   - Exchangeable base cation (Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺) increases
   - pH buffering (ΔpH = +0.2-0.5 over 3 years at 2.7 t/ha/yr)

3. **Generate net atmospheric CO₂ removal** of 0.8-2.4 tCO₂/ha/yr (conservative, field-scale) when accounting for:
   - Alkalinity export in drainage water (primary pathway)
   - Soil inorganic carbon (SIC) accumulation (secondary pathway)
   - Upstream emissions (grinding: 50 kg CO₂/t basalt; transport: 10 kg CO₂/t basalt)
   - Mineral surface passivation and secondary mineral precipitation

4. **Maintain agronomic productivity and environmental safety**, with:
   - No toxic trace metal (Ni, Cr, As) mobilization above regulatory limits
   - Beneficial nutrient addition (Mg²⁺ correction of deficiency, micronutrient supply)
   - No groundwater alkalinity pollution (saturation indices < 0 for calcite)

**H₀:** ERW application will **not** generate measurable net CO₂ removal due to:
- Mineral surface passivation limiting weathering
- Secondary carbonate precipitation within soil profile (short-circuit pathway)
- Upstream emissions exceeding CO₂ removal
- Hydrologic export of alkalinity insufficient for durable sequestration

### **1.3 Alternative Hypotheses**

**H₂ (Adverse):** ERW will cause unacceptable environmental impacts:
- Trace metal mobilization (Ni, Cr > WHO drinking water standards)
- Excessive pH increase (ΔpH > 1.0 → nutrient imbalances)
- Eutrophication from P, Si release

## **3. GEOCHEMICAL MECHANISMS & MODELING**

### **3.1 Primary Weathering Reactions**

#### **Olivine (Forsterite End-member):**
```
Mg₂SiO₄(s) + 4CO₂(aq) + 4H₂O → 2Mg²⁺ + 4HCO₃⁻ + H₄SiO₄
```
**Stoichiometry:** 1 mol Mg₂SiO₄ sequesters 4 mol CO₂ (2.51 t CO₂ / t forsterite)

#### **Clinopyroxene (Diopside End-member):**
```
CaMgSi₂O₆(s) + 4CO₂(aq) + 4H₂O → Ca²⁺ + Mg²⁺ + 4HCO₃⁻ + 2H₄SiO₄
```
**Stoichiometry:** 1 mol CaMgSi₂O₆ sequesters 4 mol CO₂ (0.81 t CO₂ / t diopside)

#### **Plagioclase (Anorthite End-member):**
```
CaAl₂Si₂O₈(s) + 2CO₂(aq) + 3H₂O → Ca²⁺ + 2HCO₃⁻ + Al₂Si₂O₅(OH)₄(s)
```
**Stoichiometry:** 1 mol CaAl₂Si₂O₈ sequesters 2 mol CO₂ (0.32 t CO₂ / t anorthite)

**Net Basalt Weathering (Simplified):**
```
Basalt (Ca,Mg silicates) + CO₂ + H₂O → Ca²⁺ + Mg²⁺ + HCO₃⁻ + SiO₂(aq) + clays
```

### **3.2 Dissolution Kinetics**

#### **Transition State Theory (TST) Rate Law:**

$$
r = k \cdot A \cdot \prod a_i^{n_i} \cdot \left(1 - \frac{Q}{K}\right)^m
$$

Where:
- $r$ = dissolution rate (mol/m²/s)
- $k$ = rate constant (function of T, pH)
- $A$ = reactive surface area (m²/g)
- $a_i$ = activity of species $i$ (H⁺, organic ligands)
- $n_i$ = reaction order
- $Q/K$ = saturation state
- $m$ = saturation state exponent (~1 for silicates)

#### **pH-Dependent Rate Law (Forsterite Example):**

$$
r_{\text{forsterite}} = k_{\text{acid}} \cdot [H^+]^{0.5} + k_{\text{neutral}} + k_{\text{base}} \cdot [OH^-]^{0.5}
$$

**Literature Values (25°C):**
- $k_{\text{acid}}$ = 1.0 × 10⁻⁶ mol/m²/s (Pokrovsky & Schott, 2000)
- $k_{\text{neutral}}$ = 1.4 × 10⁻⁹ mol/m²/s
- $k_{\text{base}}$ = 6.3 × 10⁻⁸ mol/m²/s

**At pH 5.5 (São Miguel average):**
$$
r = 1.0 \times 10^{-6} \times [10^{-5.5}]^{0.5} + 1.4 \times 10^{-9}
$$
$$
r = 1.8 \times 10^{-9} \text{ mol/m}^2/\text{s}
$$

**→ ~1,280x faster than at pH 7.0 (proton-promoted dissolution dominates)**

#### **Temperature Dependence (Arrhenius):**

$$
k(T) = k(298K) \cdot \exp\left(\frac{-E_a}{R}\left(\frac{1}{T} - \frac{1}{298}\right)\right)
$$

**For forsterite:** $E_a$ = 79 kJ/mol

**At São Miguel (T = 291K = 18°C):**
$$
k(291K) = k(298K) \times 0.73
$$

**→ ~27% slower than at 25°C (modest temperature effect)**

### **4.2 CO₂ Removal Calculation**

#### **Method 1: Stoichiometric (Conservative Upper Bound)**

$$
\text{CO}_2 \text{ (kg)} = \left(\text{MgO}_{\text{weathered}} \times \frac{44}{40}\right) + \left(\text{CaO}_{\text{weathered}} \times \frac{44}{56}\right)
$$

**Assumptions:**
- Basalt composition: 8% MgO, 10% CaO
- Weathering efficiency: $\eta$ = 45% over 10 years (from literature: Kelland et al. 2020)
- Application rate: 2.7 t/ha/yr (lime replacement scenario)

**Calculation:**
```
MgO weathered = 2,700 kg × 0.08 × 0.45 = 97.2 kg MgO/ha/yr
CaO weathered = 2,700 kg × 0.10 × 0.45 = 121.5 kg CaO/ha/yr

CO₂ from MgO = 97.2 × (44/40) = 107 kg CO₂/ha/yr
CO₂ from CaO = 121.5 × (44/56) = 95 kg CO₂/ha/yr

TOTAL = 202 kg CO₂/ha/yr = 0.20 tCO₂/ha/yr
```

**→ This is a CONSERVATIVE LOWER BOUND (45% weathering, no rate enhancement)**

#### **Method 2: Field-Enhanced Rate (Realistic Estimate)**

**Applying São Miguel enhancements:**
- pH effect: 5-12x enhancement (from Section 3.2)
- Organic matter effect: 1.5-2.0x enhancement (Drever & Stillings, 1997)
- Climate effect: 1.5-2.0x enhancement (high rainfall, moderate temperature)

**Combined enhancement:** 5 × 1.5 × 1.5 = **11.25x** (conservative midpoint)

**Adjusted weathering efficiency:** 45% × (11.25/5) = **101%** over 10 years → **Cap at 80%** (realistic maximum for 10 years)

**Recalculation:**
```
MgO weathered = 2,700 kg × 0.08 × 0.80 = 172.8 kg MgO/ha/yr
CaO weathered = 2,700 kg × 0.10 × 0.80 = 216.0 kg CaO/ha/yr

CO₂ from MgO = 172.8 × (44/40) = 190 kg CO₂/ha/yr
CO₂ from CaO = 216.0 × (44/56) = 170 kg CO₂/ha/yr

TOTAL = 360 kg CO₂/ha/yr = 0.36 tCO₂/ha/yr
```

**→ This assumes rapid near-complete weathering, needs field verification**

#### **Method 3: Alkalinity Export (Direct MRV)**

**Measure alkalinity flux in drainage water:**

$$
\text{CO}_2 \text{ removal (mol/ha/yr)} = \text{Alkalinity Export (meq/ha/yr)} / 2
$$

**Example:**
- Drainage flux: 1,000 mm/yr = 10,000 m³/ha/yr = 10 × 10⁶ L/ha/yr
- Alkalinity increase from ERW: +0.5 meq/L (conservative)
- Total alkalinity export: 0.5 meq/L × 10 × 10⁶ L/ha/yr = 5 × 10⁶ meq/ha/yr
- CO₂ removal: (5 × 10⁶ meq/ha/yr) / 2 / 1000 = **2,500 mol CO₂/ha/yr**
- **= 2,500 × 44 / 1000 = 110 kg CO₂/ha/yr = 0.11 tCO₂/ha/yr**

**→ This is MEASURABLE and VERIFIABLE (gold standard for MRV)**

## **5. ENVIRONMENTAL RISK ASSESSMENT**

### **5.1 Trace Metal Mobilization**

**Primary Concern:** Basalt contains Ni, Cr that could mobilize in acidic soils

**Regulatory Limits (WHO Drinking Water):**
- Ni: <70 µg/L
- Cr (total): <50 µg/L

**Basalt Concentration (Assumed):**
- Ni: 100 ppm (0.01%)
- Cr: 200 ppm (0.02%)

**Worst-Case Release Scenario:**
- 100% dissolution of trace metals (extremely conservative)
- 2,700 kg basalt/ha/yr × 100 ppm Ni = 270 g Ni/ha/yr
- Dilution in 10,000 m³/ha/yr drainage = 270 g / 10 × 10⁶ L = **27 µg/L**

**→ Below WHO limit (70 µg/L), but requires field verification**

**Mitigation:**
- Source rock screening (select low-Ni basalt)
- pH monitoring (keep pH >5.5 to minimize mobilization)
- Groundwater monitoring (quarterly sampling)

### **5.2 Soil pH Over-Liming Risk**

**Scenario:** Excessive pH increase → nutrient imbalances (Fe, Mn, B deficiency)

**Predicted pH Change (2.7 t/ha/yr, 3 years):**
```
Alkalinity input: 2,700 kg × 0.18 (MgO+CaO) × 0.45 (weathering) = 219 kg oxide/ha/yr
Buffer capacity: CEC × bulk density × depth = 16 cmol/kg × 0.9 g/cm³ × 20 cm = 288 meq/kg soil
Annual alkalinity: 219 kg oxide → ~4,000 meq/ha/yr
ΔpH ≈ 4,000 / (288 × 18,000 kg soil) ≈ +0.1 pH units/yr

After 3 years: ΔpH = +0.3
```

**Final pH: 5.5 + 0.3 = 5.8 (Still acidic, SAFE)**

**→ No over-liming risk at 2.7 t/ha/yr**

### **5.3 Eutrophication (P, Si Release)**

**Concern:** Enhanced weathering releases P, Si that could cause algal blooms

**Assessment:**
- Basalt P₂O₅ content: <0.5% (typically 0.2%)
- Si release: Expected, but Si is not a limiting nutrient in oceans
- P release: Minimal from basalt; soil P already present is larger source

**→ Low risk, but monitor drainage water P, Si**
