### 1.2 CO₂ Sequestration Pathways: Primary vs Secondary

**Primary Pathway (Alkalinity Export) — STRONG:**

```
Basalt weathering reaction:
  Ca,Mg-silicate + CO₂(aq) + H₂O → Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺ + HCO₃⁻ + SiO₂(aq) + clay

Export mechanism:
  1. HCO₃⁻ moves to groundwater (high rainfall = high percolation)
  2. Transported to rivers → ocean
  3. Ocean CO₂ equilibration: HCO₃⁻ ⇌ CO₃²⁻ + CO₂(g) [released at equilibrium]
  4. Permanent CO₂ removal via silicate weathering (>1000 yr timescale)
```

**Status:** ✅ **Well-established geochemistry**
- This is the basis of the Urey reaction (2.1 billion year timescale)
- São Miguel's high rainfall ensures efficient HCO₃⁻ export
- Pilot project should **measure groundwater HCO₃⁻** as primary verification metric

**Secondary Pathway (Soil Carbonate Precipitation) — CONSERVATIVE TREATMENT:**

```
Secondary reaction (undesirable):
  Ca²⁺ + 2HCO₃⁻ → CaCO₃(s) + H₂O + CO₂(g)
  
Problem: CO₂ released back to atmosphere (not permanent)
```

**Current Treatment:** ✅ **CONSERVATIVE**
- Study assumes **secondary carbonate formation is local** (soil profile)
- Not counted as permanent CDR (correct conservative approach)
- São Miguel's acidic soils (pH 5.2–6.0) are **unfavorable for calcite**
  - Saturation index for calcite is **negative** (undersaturated)
  - Unlikely to precipitate significantly at pilot scale

**Improvement Needed:** ⚠️ **Monitor in field**
- Install monitoring wells to measure soil solution pH, Ca²⁺, HCO₃⁻, DIC
- Track calcite saturation index quarterly (first 2 years critical)

### 1.3 Alkalinity Export & Long-term Carbon Accounting

**Concern:** Does alkalinity actually export, or is it retained in soil profile?

**São Miguel Mitigation Factors:**

| Factor | Status | Impact |
|--------|--------|--------|
| Rainfall | 1,750 mm/yr | ✅ HIGH → Forces percolation |
| Drainage | Year-round | ✅ HIGH → No seasonal dry-down |
| Soil depth | >60 cm | ✅ GOOD → Sufficient leaching path |
| Base saturation | 20–40% | ✅ GOOD → Not "clogged" with bases |
| Soil texture | Clay-loam | ⚠️ MODERATE → Could retain cations |

**Alkalinity Residence Time Estimate:**
```
Estimated residence time = Soil pore volume / Annual drainage
= (60 cm depth × 0.40 porosity) / (1,750 mm + ET loss)
≈ 200 mm / 950 mm effective drainage
≈ 0.2–0.3 years (2–4 months)
→ Reasonable timescale for HCO₃⁻ export
```

**Status:** ✅ **ACCEPTABLE for conservative analysis**
- Pilot project MUST measure groundwater chemistry to validate
- If export is slower, CO₂ removal will be **lower than modeled** (conservative starting point)

### 1.5 Upstream Emissions: Grinding & Transport

**Current Treatment:**

| Emission Source | Estimate | Status | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Grinding | 50 kg CO₂/t basalt | ✅ Conservative | Based on limestone grinding (typical) |
| Transport | 10 kg CO₂/t basalt | ✅ Conservative | Assumes 100 km by truck |
| **Total upstream** | **60 kg CO₂/t** | **✅ 12% of uptake** | Deducted from gross sequestration |

**Validation:**

```
Example (Lime replacement scenario):
  Gross CO₂ from weathering:  1.7 t/ha/yr = 1,700 kg/ha/yr
  Minus grinding emissions:   (2,700 kg basalt × 0.050) = 135 kg CO₂/ha/yr
  Minus transport emissions:  (2,700 kg basalt × 0.010) = 27 kg CO₂/ha/yr
  Net CO₂ removal:           1,700 - 135 - 27 = 1,538 kg CO₂/ha/yr ≈ 1.5 t/ha/yr ✅
```

**Status:** ✅ **CONSERVATIVE & TRANSPARENT**
- Emissions are **explicitly subtracted** (not ignored)
- Estimates are **conservative** (actual grinding may be more efficient)
- Could be improved by **local quarry** (reduce transport distance)

## 2. CRITICAL SCIENTIFIC ASSUMPTIONS & CAVEATS

### Assumption 1: pH-Dependent Weathering Rate
**Formula Used:** `multiplier = 10^(7-pH) / 10^(7-7)`

**Justification:** Simplified TST (Transition State Theory) for silicate dissolution
**Caveat:** This is **simplified** for rapid field estimation
**Better approach:** Use full TST with temperature dependence
**Impact on results:** Should use sensitivity analysis (see Section 3)

### Assumption 2: Weathering Efficiency = 45%
**Interpretation:** 45% of basalt will weather to release soluble cations over 10 years
**Justification:** Based on field weathering studies in tropical/subtropical regions
**Caveat:** Actual efficiency depends on **particle size, rainfall timing, soil aeration**
**Recommendation:** **Sensitivity analysis critical** (20–70% range realistic)

### Assumption 3: 1,750 mm Annual Rainfall
**Source:** São Miguel average
**Caveat:** **Highly variable** (1,500–2,000 mm across island)
**Impact:** Direct linear effect on weathering rate
**Recommendation:** **Model dry vs wet years** separately

### Assumption 4: No Secondary Carbonate Short-Circuiting
**Assumption:** HCO₃⁻ escapes soil before converting to CO₃²⁻ or CaCO₃
**Caveat:** Could be wrong if soil pH rises rapidly
**Control:** **Monthly pH monitoring in first year** (critical)

### Assumption 5: 50% Basalt Recovery Factor
**Assumption:** Only 50% of in-situ basalt can be extracted sustainably
**Caveat:** Could be higher (70%) or lower (30%)
**Impact on island-scale:** ±40% variation in total CDR potential
**Recommendation:** **Geological survey required** before commercial scaling

### 3.2 Uncertainty Bounds (95% Confidence)

2. **Upstream emissions:** ±15% (grinding may be more efficient)

**Combined uncertainty (quadrature):**
$$ \Delta CO_2 = CO_2 \times \sqrt{0.30^2 + 0.15^2 + 0.20^2 + 0.20^2} ≈ ±42\% $$

   - Frequency: Quarterly (first 2 years), then annually

   - Frequency: Quarterly (first 2 years)
   - Uncertainty: ±5%

4. ✅ **Basalt weathering rate (mass loss)**
   - Method: Bury mesh bags, measure weathering surfaces
   - Frequency: Annual
   - Uncertainty: ±15%
